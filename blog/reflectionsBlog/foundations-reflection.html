What are three new things you have learnt about yourself and your ego as a result of the core learning?


Three things I have learnt about myslef are:
1. I am more comfortable than I thought making space for my neurodivergent mind/learning style than I thought I was. Even at the expense of doing a task ‘incorrectly’.
2. My alternate learning style, although challenging for myself at times, can be a strength in terms of pattern-recognition.
3. There are people making space for people like me in the industry…
well, this was more based on my interactions with my facilitator than the specific core learning, but I suppose our conversations were inadvertently part of of my core learning, even if it wasn’t in the handbook.
I find it difficult to locate my ‘ego’; I think my autism means that I don’t understand a lot of the value judgements neurotypical culture places on comparison and being ‘more successful’ than others.
I learnt a little about other people’s egos; And how I can safely negotiate space for myself in situations where other peoples comparisons or value judgements of me may be skewed by their own self-perceived intelligence or need to appear important…
I suppose that ‘ego’ (originally) was used to describe the part of the mind that mediates between the unconscious and conscious performance of our body…. In which case, I could say that I learnt that there are some academic situations where I can remove some of my ‘mask’ safely…
But if ego is being used in it’s more colloquially relevant way, I would say that I have a very different way of experiencing social interaction to many, and I don’t feel particularly prideful or like I need to be ‘more successful’ or ‘more intelligent’ than anyone… I am just putting my head downing and learning and I really hope we all succeed.

What has surprised you the most about the core learning?


recently discovered I have extreme dyslexia and dyscalculia, and I am surprised that it took me so long to pick up on it——I was reminded of how difficult my experiences of classroom learning was by having to interact with highly technical written language for the first time in a long while.
I was also pleasantly surprised by the support I received around my learning difference and how I didn’t feel judged; And was even encouraged to ‘break the rules’ in terms of doing what I was asked, if it supported my learning, or was more honest to me (eg. I went a little ‘off book’ on my blog posts to reflect my experiences as a neurodivergent person and felt not only accepted in doing that, but it was practically celebrated.
It was exciting not to have to ‘hide’ or assimilate into material that may not be specifically meant to me; But also not coddled….I was simply allowed to make space for myself, without everyone else having to change their learning styles to accommodate me.


were the most challenging aspects of the core learning?


I found it challenging doing some heavy analyses of my thinking while undergoing diagnostic processes of ADHD and ASD for the first time (and only recently considering that I may be someone with ASD rather than an extremely traumatised neurotypical person). I do not think it was a bad thing to be challenged, but it was hard at times to examine the struggles I have with learning and being heard, and communicating them honestly.


Why do you think that we, a programming school, are spending so much time focusing on core learning in a web development bootcamp course?


It is the responsible thing to do.
The programming world has excluded particular groups of people for a long time and so contributing to that culture requires some decisions around what you would like to contribute; Sending people into the world and giving them the tools to be heard and amplify certain voices through the language of code, means you can contribute to a culture on a way the disrupts or supports the ‘status quo’.
I intend for instance, to use this opportunity to support other queer, neurodivergent people (and women) in the coding industry…when I have the tools to.

Does the time you spent studying core learning here feel like a waste of time? Should you have just used that time to practise programming instead? Justify your answer.


I have ADHD so I get agitated easily and impatient… I also think that some of the material may have been unnecessary or not directly useful to me personally (because I already go to therapy sessions and my neurodivergence means that some of the ideas are already things I have intimate experience with)…
In saying that, it was helpful to create space for myself within the core learning and ‘defy’ my previous instinct of assimilating or appearing to assimilate when it came to conversations around the mind…it was very freeing.
I also think that irregardless of what parts I feel were personally helpful to me, it is something that is so necessary and made me feel a lot more comfortable with Dev academy in general.
Because, I would rather be in a classroom that makes empathy matter as much as anything else on the learning curriculum.
I think there is something to be said for not every question needing to be aimed at you, for it to be a question worth asking.
I felt like I could find a productive and honest way to answer the questions or use the time, even if it meant I strayed a little from what was being asked of me…
So, er, no, it was not a waste of time; But I am particularly grateful for it being part of the curriculum for those who may be unfamiliar with concepts of plasticity/empathy/boundry-setting/listening in an appropriate and respectful way, and may be being introduced to those things for the first time, or made to pay attention to them at the same time.
To me the tech content was all new and unfamiliar, and the core stuff was a little more familiar ; I think the core content needs to destabilise people who enter and are only comfortable and confident with tech, in the same way that the tech component needs to destabilise and challenge me…
I think that the people who would find this stuff really challenging (for other reasons than me) need to experience that discomfort and those growing pains and it was helpful for me in a more peripheral, abstract, playful way where it let me test some boundaries and ways of making space for my alternative thinking and learning styles.
