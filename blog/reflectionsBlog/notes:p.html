Q: In this exploration, did anything surprise you? Change for you?


I was surprised by how uncomfortable was with the language I encountered in my research…
And terribly surprised that rather than be dishonest about my experience, choose to make space for myself and my experiences, even at the risk of offending others.
My experiences around ‘thought hacking’ and ‘optimised learning’ have mostly involved feeling abnormal or left-out, because my mind works so differently to the socialised norm, and this has made me a target of a lot of bad treatment.
I think something I realised, was that, although I have spent years researching the human-brain, epigenetic, memory ect. I am still hugely uncomfortable with the language that we use to talk about learning, intelligence and brain-function.
It often leaves out or passes value-judgement on the neurodivergent ——or, might just bring up bad memories and experiences for some.
I came across a few ideas I disagreed with ——Not because I don’t believe they would be unhelpful for others, but because they were not very helpful for me personally, because learning and brain-function is so personal, and often texts on it cater to ‘typical’ brain functioning;
A lot of sources implied a didactic singular way to manipulate the plasticity of your mind, that ‘increased brain function’ (as though that is possible!). And there was a focus on intelligence and things like IQ, which troublingly has it’s origins in eugenics and measures ‘intelligence’ according to a very narrow margin of typical presentation….usually a type of intelligence that means it’s possessor can contribute to capitalism and be a ‘successful, productive’, producing (and consuming) member of society….
So, I found that frustrating.
Any article that claims to know ‘how the mind works’ and does not mention the room for difference isn’t particularly interesting to me, though I see the importance to those who don’t consider themselves capable of change or find it difficult to empathise with the fluidity of experience others may exhibit.

I suppose it is the first time I have recognised feeling excluded from an idea and clocking that it is because a lot of the texts and discussions around it reflect a dominant ideology of normality that I do not experience and there for it was not a matter of me not ‘fitting’ the material, but the material not fitting me.
I enjoyed pushing some boundries, and risking being honest… I wait with bated breathe to see how this is received and will finish by saying, that I don’t expect every learning-excersise to fit me specifically, and I know others may find things useful that I do not… But I think it is cool to communicate why I might feel a material isn’t aimed at me, or where my discomfort comes from.

How will you integrate growth mindset into your learning journey?


I will not integrate this learning in particular…even though I understand how focusing on/researching these topics would benefit others.
I spent five years intensely researching cognitive function and ways to manipulate the mind/memory when I was diagnosed with C-PTSD…and, well, I went a bit too deep.
So now, I try to relax and not be too specific about how I learn.
I have dyslexia, ASD and ADHD…so of-cores I will continue to find ways to support my learning and affirm my ability to learn…but reading sources on how my brain works is something that I have hyper-fixated on in an attempt to ‘cure’ my neuro-divergance, so I will treat them with caution.
Reflect and discuss how these understandings may shape or influence your learning plan and strategy.
I have recently endeavoured not to ‘mask’ my autism, and this includes pointing out when something might not fit me or be aimed at me.
My learning strategy continues to be making space for myself, no matter how nerve-wracking it is to point-out my difference or the way I might have an unusual response to things others might be comfortable with.
